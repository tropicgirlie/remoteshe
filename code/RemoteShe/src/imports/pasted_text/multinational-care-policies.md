# Multinational companies with strong care infrastructure policies

**48 multinationals across seven industries meet most or all of the criteria for leading care infrastructure**: 20+ weeks paid maternity leave, IVF/fertility benefits, childcare support, and flexible or remote work policies. The strongest performers — Spotify, Diageo, Etsy, S&P Global, and HPE — offer fully gender-equal parental leave of 26 weeks, comprehensive fertility coverage, and genuine work flexibility. The landscape is shifting fast: many companies expanded benefits significantly between 2022 and 2025, while remote work policies have tightened at several tech giants despite care benefits remaining strong.

Each entry below includes all available verified data points. Items marked ⚠️ are self-reported or unverified. Benefits often vary by country — figures reflect global minimums or US-specific policies as noted.

## Notable patterns and emerging trends

Three structural shifts define the current care infrastructure landscape. First, **gender-neutral leave is now table stakes** at leading multinationals — Etsy, S&P Global, HPE, Spotify, Automattic, and Lululemon all offer identical leave regardless of gender or caregiver role, with no "primary/secondary" distinction. Diageo's equalization drove men's average paternity uptake from 23 to 105 days, demonstrating that policy design directly shapes behavior.

Second, **fertility benefits have escalated dramatically** since 2020. Bank of America's unlimited IVF coverage, Google's $75,000 lifetime budget, Spotify's $60,000 via Carrot, and EY's $50,000 "Pathways to Parenthood" set a high bar. Progyny and Carrot have become the dominant platforms — Progyny powers fertility benefits at Meta, Salesforce, and Airbnb, while Carrot serves Spotify, Netflix, and Stripe. Datadog stands out for offering **$23,000/year across three separate care wallets** (fertility, adoption, reproductive care).

Third, **remote work policies are diverging sharply** from care benefit generosity. Google, Meta, Apple, and JPMorgan have tightened to 3–5 days in-office even as they maintain best-in-class parental and fertility benefits. Meanwhile, Spotify, Airbnb, Automattic, and GitLab maintain genuine work-from-anywhere policies. Spotify credits its WFA policy with **15% lower attrition** and improved diversity. Companies serious about care infrastructure increasingly recognize that flexibility is itself a care benefit — the ability to manage childcare logistics around asynchronous work may matter more than weeks of formal leave.

Among lesser-known standouts, **Schneider Electric** deserves particular attention for applying its Global Family Leave Policy across all 100+ countries from day one of employment with no tenure requirement. **Wise** standardized 18-week gender-equal parental leave across all 17 markets, raising Malaysia from 9 to 18 weeks far above local law. **SAS Institute's** legendary onsite daycare (600+ children since 1981) and **Patagonia's** 40-year-old childcare program (with 100% maternal retention) remain the gold standards for employer-provided childcare, proving that sustained investment in care infrastructure delivers measurable business returns.

## Conclusion

The strongest care infrastructure employers share a common pattern: they treat parental support as a system rather than a single benefit. The true leaders — Diageo, Spotify, Etsy, S&P Global, Salesforce, and Deloitte UK — combine generous gender-equal leave with fertility coverage, childcare support, flexible work, and thoughtful return-to-work transitions. The most innovative policies are not just about duration of leave: HPE's 36-month part-time transition, Etsy's 2-year flexible leave window, Cisco's grandparent leave, and Danone's "1,000-day" pregnancy-to-toddlerhood philosophy represent the frontier. Companies that integrate care as a genuine infrastructure investment — rather than a checkbox benefit — consistently report higher retention, faster return-to-work rates, and stronger gender representation in leadership.
